Questions and answers about our data, methodology, and how to use it

Methodology: How to read and use this data

Governments invest public money via subsidies in solar energy to support the green transition and promote equity, but it is often hard to see where this funding goes and who benefits. This project makes solar subsidy and installation data transparent by bringing it into a single, interactive dashboard, helping users understand how photovoltaic (PV) investments are distributed over time and across communities, including low-income areas.

  1. 1. What is this project and why is it valuable?

    Sunlight Ledger makes solar subsidy allocation and installation data transparent and auditable. Governments at all levels use subsidies to incentivize solar adoption, but the public often struggles to see where the money goes and who benefits. This project answers three core questions:

    • Where are subsidy dollars and installations concentrated? ZIP-level mapping shows geographic distribution.
    • How much capacity has been built and where? Total kW metrics reveal installed capacity hotspots.
    • To what extent do low-income communities benefit? Equity metrics track funding and deployment in disadvantaged areas.

    By making these data defensible and linked to official sources, we enable journalists to investigate patterns, help taxpayers track public spending, and support policymakers in designing equitable programs.

  2. 2. Who is this data for?

    This project serves three main audiences:

    • Journalists: Use the dashboard to find stories about subsidy concentration, identify anomalies (ZIP codes with unusually high costs per kilowatt), and verify claims with source data. Example: "90% of low-income solar funding went to five ZIP codes—here's the map."
    • Taxpayers and advocates: Track how public funds are allocated over time, compare equity across regions, and hold elected officials accountable. Example: "Our county received $X in solar subsidies; here's how much went to low-income residents."
    • City and county sustainability planners: Identify gaps in solar deployment, benchmark performance against neighboring areas, and design targeted programmes for underserved communities. Example: "North district lags in per-capita solar adoption—here's a proposal to address it."

  3. 3. How is the data constructed and interpreted?

    Data is aggregated from official sources and organized by ZIP code (typically year-by-year). Individual solar projects are rolled up into aggregate metrics at the ZIP level. Below are the metric categories and what they show:

    Total Metrics
    • Total subsidy dollars per ZIP code — Shows where public solar funding is geographically concentrated.
    • Total installed solar capacity (kW) — Shows where solar generation capacity has been built across ZIP codes.
    • Total number of solar installations — Shows overall participation levels across ZIP codes.
    Low-Income Metrics
    • Subsidy dollars allocated to low-income projects — Shows how much funding is directed to low-income communities within each ZIP code.
    • Installed solar capacity in low-income projects (kW) — Shows how much solar capacity has been built in low-income projects by ZIP code.
    • Number of low-income solar installations — Shows deployment activity in low-income areas across ZIP codes.
    Equity Metrics (computed)
    • Share of total installations in low-income areas — (low_projects / project_count) — Shows whether low-income communities are proportionally participating in solar adoption.
    • Share of total installed capacity in low-income areas — (low_income_kW / total_kW) — Shows whether capacity deployment is equitably distributed across communities.
    • Share of total subsidy dollars going to low-income projects — (low_income_subsidy / total_subsidy) — Shows how subsidy allocation aligns with equity goals.
    Efficiency and Intensity Metrics (computed)
    • Subsidy dollars per installation ($/install) — (total_subsidy / project_count) — Shows the average incentive amount per solar installation in a ZIP code.
    • Subsidy dollars per kilowatt ($/kW) — (total_subsidy / total_kW) — Shows how much public funding is associated with each unit of installed capacity.

    Normalization across metrics lets users compare intensity and allocation patterns across ZIP codes and time periods. A ZIP with high subsidy per kW but low total capacity may signal cost overruns or targeting of expensive installations.

  4. 4. What are the data sources and coverage?

    We draw from authoritative, publicly available sources maintained by California state agencies:

    California Energy Commission (CEC)
    California GIS Portal – Distributed Generation Database
    Tracks individual distributed generation (solar) projects, installation dates, capacity, and location.

    California Distributed Generation Statistics
    California DG Stats
    Aggregated state-level statistics on solar adoption, equipment, and incentive programmes.

    Data is refreshed periodically as official sources update. See the dashboard footer or README for the most recent data publication date.

  5. 5. Which low-income programmes are covered and what do they do?

    This project focuses on California's flagship low-income solar incentive programmes:

    SASH (Single-family Affordable Solar Homes)
    Learn more at Grid Alternatives
    Provides free or subsidized solar installations to low-income homeowners, reducing upfront costs and enabling long-term savings.

    DAC-SASH (Disadvantaged Communities – SASH)
    Learn more at Grid Alternatives
    Targets communities disproportionately burdened by environmental and economic disadvantage; covers installation and equipment costs.

    MASH (Multifamily Affordable Solar Housing)
    Learn more at Grid Alternatives
    Supports solar on multifamily buildings serving low-income residents; reduces shared utility costs.

    Common features of these programmes:

    • State-supported and administered (California Energy Commission, California Public Utilities Commission).
    • Installation-linked subsidies (cap up-front costs; ongoing operation and maintenance funded separately).
    • Linked to a physical location and ZIP code, enabling geographic analysis.
    • Designed to reduce energy burden and support long-term wealth building in underserved communities.
