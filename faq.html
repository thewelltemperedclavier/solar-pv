Frequently asked questions

Governments subsidize solar PV (photovoltaic) to accelerate the green transition, reduce pressure on energy infrastructure, and support equitable access to clean energy.

The Single-family Affordable Solar Homes program (SASH 1.0 & 2.0) provides solar incentives for owner-occupied, low-income households.

The Multi-family Affordable Solar Housing program (MASH 1.0 & 2.0) provides incentives for solar installations on qualifying multi-family affordable housing properties.

The Disadvantaged Communities - Single-family Affordable Solar Homes program is the successor to SASH, specifically targeting homeowners in the top 25% most disadvantaged communities in California.

Eligibility is defined by properties qualifying as affordable housing or households meeting annual income limits set by the California Alternate Rates for Energy (CARE) or Family Electric Rate Assistance (FERA) guidelines.

PG&E, SCE, and SDG&E are the three primary California Investor Owned Utilities (IOUs) that manage the Pacific Gas & Electric, Southern California Edison, and San Diego Gas & Electric service territories. These companies are responsible for collecting and reporting the interconnection data used in this project to track solar installations across the state.

The Low-Income dataset only includes projects that received a specific state subsidy (SASH, MASH, or DAC-SASH). General market solar projects are found in the larger interconnection datasets.

The Low-Income incentive data is updated weekly on Thursday mornings. The broader interconnection market data is updated monthly.

This project focuses exclusively on the modern era of state-funded low-income solar initiatives: MASH, SASH, and DAC-SASH.

All analysis is based on the Completion Date (when the system was physically installed and finalized), not the date it was approved. This ensures the transparency site reflects actual shovels in the ground and not just paperwork.

Because the Incentive data and Market data live in separate databases, we link them using ZIP codes as the common denominator. By aggregating both datasets to the ZIP level, we can compare how much public money was spent in a neighborhood versus the total solar activity in that same area.

In 2006, California passed Assembly Bill (AB) 2723, which mandated that at least 10% of all state solar funds must benefit low-income properties. Later, AB 217 (2013) extended these programs with an additional $108 million to ensure solar was not just for wealthy homeowners.

Launched in 2019, DAC-SASH targets the top 25% most disadvantaged communities in the state. Beyond installing panels, it is required to provide job training on every project to help residents in these communities enter the solar workforce.

These programs are overseen by the California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) and are funded by the customers of the state’s large electric utilities (PG&E, SCE, and SDG&E).

The architecture converts individual records into two structured summaries optimized for time-series analysis and neighborhood-level benchmarking.
